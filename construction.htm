Cabinet Construction:

After spending a few weeks ringing Arcade suppliers and looking around at second hand cabinets, I decided that I couldn't really get what I wanted out of one. I wanted my beast to be big and imposing, and the ones that suited my purpose were outside my budget. It became apparent that it would be cheaper to build one myself rather than buy an old shell. Boy, was that wrong!

The whole thing is built out of 18mm MDF sheets and good old 2x4. It stands approx 2 metres tall and about 80cm wide, which looked ok in the garage during construction, but our house has a lower ceiling than the garage, so it dominates the room inside.

I cut most of the pieces I needed using a jigsaw and sanded them back to the lines I'd drawn. This was a very slow method, but the best I could manage without a full tool shop behind me. The frame is screwed together using big hex bolt (allen key) wood screws, and I've got an allen key adapter for my cordless drill, so the whole thing can be disassembled/reassembled in about an hour or so. Just as well, 'cos it doesn't fit through any doorways! Think I'll be leaving it where it is for a while.

The front door is mounted on piano hinge with a keyed lock, so I can keep my brother's kids out of the guts. There is no access via the back panel unless you take it off.

Control Panel:

The Control Panel is held in place by 4 coach bolts, 2 horizontal and 2 vertical, screwed through the 2x4 supports. This prevents it from moving at all, even during the most aggressive gameplay.

Seeing as I had one lying around, I decided to get another Sidewinder game pad and hack the two of them to provide input for the joysticks and buttons. The Sidewinders have 10 button inputs each, so hooking them both up provided me with 20 usable buttons and 2 joysticks. The Sidewinder is also easily recognised by Windows, so there were no configuration and calibration hassles. See the links page.

I wanted to include a sliding keyboard drawer in the Control Panel, but I must've got my measurements wrong and the one I had didn't fit once the joysticks and buttons were installed. They have a low clearance, so if you intend to do the same thing, allow plenty of vertical room. The front door is also mounted on piano hinge with a keyed lock.

The joysticks are Happ Supers, and the buttons and microswitches are all from Happ Controls as well. I ordered them through The Real Bob Roberts (see the links page). This guy gives great service, I highly recommend him. The two smaller red buttons are for Escape and Insert Coin, and were cheapos I got from local electronics stores.

I intend to add a spinner at some stage (most likely the Slikstik Tornado) for games like Arkanoid and Tempest, and possibly an ACT Labs Light Gun as well for shooting games.

Finishing touches:

Cabinet power is provided by a surge-protected powerboard inside the cab, which is wired to a standard PC power connector mounted in the back panel. This connector also has a fused switch, which means I can plug the cabinet in with a single PC power cable. The large green and red buttons mounted under the Control Panel are wired into the motherboard's 'Power Switch' and 'Reset' pins. This way, I can flick the switch at the back to activate cabinet power (PC, monitor, marquee etc) and then press the green button to fire up the PC.

Power Connector/Switch

The monitor glass is standard 6mm window glass; the reverse side has been spray-painted black, with the monitor window area masked off. This looks very effective, and is really simple to do.

My wife handled all of the spray-painting, and a couple of coats on everything came up trumps. I had looked into laminates, but they were way too expensive, as was T-Molding for the edges. Maybe sometime in the future, you never know ...

The marquee had to be specially made for me by a sign company. I originally intended to get a print shop to print it out and mount it myself between two sheets of thin glass, but no print shop could handle the dimensions. It was a very expensive way to do it, but the result speaks for itself.
